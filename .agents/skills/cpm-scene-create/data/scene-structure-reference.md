# Scene Structure Reference

> Loaded while shaping a scene's spine — its Setting, Narrative Purpose, and Emotional Arc —
> before the beats are broken out. It names the common scene types, the patterns an emotional
> arc tends to follow, and what a Narrative Purpose has to accomplish to earn its place. The
> examples come from *The Second Receipt* to show the method; the shapes generalize to any
> production. A scene that does not move something and does not serve a theme is a scene to cut.

---

## What a scene is here

A scene is a contiguous run of beats that share **one setting**, **one narrative function**, and
**one arc movement** — and every beat in it is exactly one shard. The brief's job is to make that
spine explicit before a single shard is generated: where we are (Setting), what the scene does
for the story (Narrative Purpose), and what it moves emotionally (Emotional Arc). The beats then
deliver that spine, one shard at a time. If you cannot state the function and the movement in a
sentence each, the scene is not ready to break into beats.

---

## Scene types

These are working archetypes, not a rulebook — most scenes lean toward one. Each type carries a
typical arc shape and a typical job, noted so the three sections of the brief stay coherent with
one another.

| Type | What it does | Usually on camera | Arc tendency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Establishing / State** | Sets the world's law and a character's starting condition; often a single figure performing a self-defining ritual, little or no dialogue | One character | Hold-and-Wind |
| **Threshold / First-Contact** | Two forces or worlds meet for the first time; the film's central metaphor made literal | Two opposed characters | Hairline-Crack |
| **Confrontation / Negotiation** | Two standing forces push against each other; dialogue-driven; a contest of tempos | Two or more | Reversal, or escalation |
| **Discovery / Turn** | A single revelation pivots the story; the moment a known fact changes meaning | One or two | Reversal begins |
| **Climax / Break** | The controlling structure breaks; a mask comes off, a choice is made | The protagonist, often with the catalyst | Collapse / Transformation |
| **Resolution / Echo** | The aftermath ripples outward; a planted theme pays off | Often new or peripheral figures | Settle |

**Grounded notes.**

- **Establishing / State** — *The Functional Ghost* (the opening scene). Elias alone in the
  sealed sedan, no dialogue partner on camera, performing the armoring ritual that defines his
  Cold state. Its work is to calibrate the audience to exactly what the warmth to come will be up
  against. State scenes are behavioral, not conversational: the character *is* the exposition.
- **Threshold / First-Contact** — *The Threshold* (the door scene). The Cold world crosses into
  the Warm world for the first time; the door frame is the film's central line made literal.
  First-contact scenes turn on a single event — here, a name spoken before a script can be
  delivered.
- **Confrontation / Negotiation, Discovery / Turn, Climax / Break, Resolution / Echo** — these sit
  later in the arc: the visit becoming a contest between Elias's deadlines and Mara's legato; the
  discovery of the Second Receipt, when a legal debt collides with a moral one; the breaking of
  the Collector's Mask when Elias uses his own checkbook against the System; and the closing echo
  of the girl in the park — the living proof the kindness rippled. Each is named in the
  production's arc; the type tells you what the scene must *accomplish*, which sets its arc and
  its purpose.

A scene can blend types — a confrontation that turns into a discovery — but a brief should know
which one is primary, because that drives the arc and the beats.

## Emotional-arc patterns — Opens at / Closes at

Every brief states where the scene **Opens at** and where it **Closes at**. Two rules govern the
pair:

1. **Name a state, not an event.** "Sterile Isolation (Absolute Zero)" is a state; "he scans a
   file" is an event. The arc is the emotional reading of the scene, not its plot.
2. **The endpoints must differ.** A scene exists to move something. Even a hairline move counts —
   but Opens and Closes reading the same, for a character who is supposed to change, means the
   scene has no engine. Find the movement, or merge the scene into its neighbor.

Strong arcs fall into a few shapes:

| Pattern | The movement | Use when | Grounded illustration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hold-and-Wind** | Same direction, rising intensity — the spring winds without changing direction | Calibrating a state, or loading tension before a later turn | Opens at *Sterile Isolation (Absolute Zero)* → Closes at *Pressurized Anticipation (The Coiled Spring)* |
| **Hairline-Crack** | Maximum control to the first real crack — a tiny delta that still moves | A threshold or first contact | Opens at *Functional Ghost — 100% intact* → Closes at *First Fracture* |
| **Reversal / Thaw** | One pole to its opposite — the scene flips a state | A genuine turn or thaw | A guarded state opening that closes genuinely reached |
| **Collapse / Break** | Controlled to broken or transformed | The climax, where the structure gives way | A held composure opening that closes broken open |
| **Flat Anchor** | Opens and closes at the same state — by design | A flat-arc character whose job is to move *someone else* | Mara holds at *Grounded Acceptance* throughout; she initiates the change rather than undergoing it |

**Keep the arc honest to the beats.** The Opens/Closes pair is the scale; the beats' State
Change deltas are the weights on it. If the beats move a character only a hairline — say a 0% to
2% shift — the Closes-at should read as a first crack, not a thaw. An arc that promises more than
the beats deliver, or less, is a brief at war with itself.

**Flat-arc characters are the exception to the must-move rule.** A character who enters the story
already complete — already at 85% and steady — does not change across a scene; she changes
*others*. When such a character is the only one on camera, name the arc against what the scene
moves in the audience or in the absent protagonist, not against her. When she shares the frame
with a changing character, the scene's arc belongs to the one who moves.

## Narrative-function notes

**Narrative Purpose states what the scene accomplishes for the story — not what happens in it.**
"Establishes the full weight of the Cold ... calibrating the audience to exactly what the warmth
will be up against" is a function. "Elias scans a file and walks to a door" is a plot summary; it
tells you the events but not why the scene exists.

Every scene must do at least one of five things:

- **Establish** — set a world rule, a character state, or a relationship for the first time.
- **Escalate** — raise the pressure on a standing situation.
- **Turn** — pivot the story; change what a known fact means.
- **Pay off** — deliver on something planted earlier.
- **Echo** — let a consequence ripple outward.

The clean test: **if you cut the scene, does the story still parse?** If it does, the scene is not
earning its shards — fold it into a neighbor or drop it. A scene that establishes *and* escalates
is doing honest work; a scene that does neither is a deleted scene that has not been deleted yet.

**Serves theme.** The brief also names at least one of the production's themes the scene advances,
and how. Check the name against the Show Bible so you are serving a real pillar of the story, not
inventing one. *The Second Receipt* runs on three:

- **Transactional vs. Relational Wealth** — the gap between what a bank statement says and what a
  life actually holds.
- **The "Good Soldier" Complex** — the moral cost of "just doing my job."
- **Invisible Echoes** — how one quiet kindness ripples through a community unseen.

A scene may serve a primary and a secondary theme; name the primary first. The opening State
scene serves the Good Soldier Complex first (every beat is an act of self-suppression) and
Transactional vs. Relational Wealth second (a human life reduced to a file number). A scene that
serves no nameable theme is decoration — beautiful, maybe, but it is not carrying the film.

---

## How the three cohere

The Setting, the type, the arc, and the purpose should agree. Use this as a sanity pass, not a
formula — a threshold scene *can* hold-and-wind, a state scene *can* crack — but when they
diverge, ask whether the scene knows what it is.

| Scene type | Tends to open / close as | Tends to do |
|---|---|---|
| Establishing / State | Hold-and-Wind | Establish |
| Threshold / First-Contact | Hairline-Crack | Establish + Turn |
| Confrontation / Negotiation | Reversal or escalation | Escalate |
| Discovery / Turn | Reversal | Turn |
| Climax / Break | Collapse / Break | Pay off |
| Resolution / Echo | Settle | Echo |

When the type, the arc, and the purpose point the same way, the beats almost write themselves —
each one a step from where the scene opens to where it closes, in service of the one thing the
scene is there to do.
