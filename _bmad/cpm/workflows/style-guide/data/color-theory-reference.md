# Color Theory Reference

Reference data for the Visual Architect during color palette creation.

## Color Relationships

| Relationship | Definition | Effect |
|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Complementary | Opposite on color wheel | High contrast, vibrant tension |
| Analogous | Adjacent on color wheel | Harmony, cohesion, subtle variation |
| Triadic | Three evenly spaced colors | Balance with richness |
| Split-Complementary | Base + two adjacent to complement | Contrast with less tension |
| Monochromatic | Shades/tints of one hue | Cohesive, mood-dominant |

## Common Cinematic Palettes

### Orange and Teal (Hollywood Standard)
- Warm skin tones against cool backgrounds
- Creates natural depth through complementary contrast
- Risk: Overused, can feel generic

### Desaturated with Accent
- Predominantly muted/grey palette with one saturated color
- The accent color carries all symbolic weight
- Examples: Schindler's List (red coat), Sin City (selective color)

### Neon Noir
- Deep blacks with saturated neon accents (#FF1493, #00FFFF, #9B30FF)
- Urban, nocturnal, artificial light dominant
- Examples: Blade Runner 2049, Drive, John Wick

### Earth Tones
- Browns, greens, warm yellows, muted oranges
- Organic, grounded, historical
- Examples: The Revenant, No Country for Old Men

### Pastel/Washed
- Lifted blacks, reduced saturation, soft colors
- Nostalgic, dreamlike, memory
- Examples: Moonrise Kingdom, The Grand Budapest Hotel

### Monochrome Tinted
- Essentially B&W with a color wash
- Period feel, uniformity, mood dominance
- Examples: Traffic (color-coded storylines), The Matrix (green tint)

## Forbidden Color Reasoning Examples

Common reasons to ban specific colors:
- "Bright saturated yellow breaks the noir tension — too cheerful"
- "Pastel pink contradicts the gritty realism of the production"
- "Pure white highlights feel digital/uncinematic — use off-white tints instead"
- "Warm oranges conflict with the cold, isolating color temperature"
- "Saturated primary colors feel cartoonish in this context"
